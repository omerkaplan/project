Thoughts on building products 'Zero to one'

20 October, 2021 2 minutes read

Lately I've been having a lot of conversations around the concept of 'building products zero to one', some in the context of interviewing candidates for product management roles and the inclusion of 'building zero to one' as a requirement in the job description; and some in the context of communicating the idea of product innovation and change management to executive leadership.

The more I talk about it, the more I realize that there is a misalignment in how different people in different functions perceive the concept of 'zero to one'. My goal in writing this post is to organize my own thoughts around it and use that as reference for future conversations.

sub headline

When people talk to me about building products 'zero to one' they are often riffing on Peter Thiel and Blake Masters' 2014 thought provoking book with the same title.

One of the key takeaways from the book specifically related to the 'zero to one' concept is (and I'm paraphrasing a bit) that in order to create significant breakthroughs that would enable product to reach the 'one to many' scale-up status, a startup must create a different, valuable product that will enable it to create and capture new markets - all that packaged into a concept of 'zero to one'. To me, this is very similar to the concept of finding 'sustainable competitive advantage' presented by Richard Rumelt in 'Good Strategy, Bad strategy1'.

Since becoming popular in 2014 the concept of 'zero to one' evolved with times and requirements and when talking about it with product people these days, their definition of building a product 'zero to one' is creating something of of nothing: Whether it's an established company or a startup, take an idea that wasn't a part of the core ecosystem and using a variety of skills (research, data analysis, design, coding, communication etc) create a valuable product for said company. Literally zero to one.
